A clean credit report can mean a higher credit score, lower interest rates and a better lifestyle.

The Fair Credit Reporting Act (“FCRA”), which is encoded in 15 U.S.C. § 1681 et seq., is federal legislation enacted to promote the accuracy, fairness, and privacy in consumer credit reports. The California Consumer Credit Reporting Agencies Act (“CCRAA”) is the California version of the FCRA. The purpose of the FCRA and the CCRAA is to protect people from the transmission of inaccurate information about them and to establish credit reporting practices that utilize accurate, relevant, and current information in a confidential and responsible manner. We will use these laws to enforce your rights.

Creditors, debt collectors, and debt buyers must comply with the FCRA and CCRAA. If they don’t, you may have a claim against them. You may be entitled to recover money damages, seek injunctive relief, and in certain situations, recover statutory damages. The FCRA and CCRAA also allows for reasonable attorney’s fees, which means that if YOU prevail, they must pay your reasonable attorney’s fees.

    • UNFAIR DEBT COLLECTION

    Are You Being Contacted, Harassed or Abused By Debt Collectors?

    Millions of Americans are suffering from overwhelming debts caused by a loss of a job, divorce, illness or unexpected medical bills. Falling behind on your debts is stressful enough without the harassing debt collectors. As pointed out below, there are powerful federal and state laws which protect you from harassing collection practices and which allows us to help stop the harassing telephone calls, letters, emails, and in some cases legal action filed against you to collect a debt.

    Both, the Federal Debt Collection Practices Act (“FDCPA”) and the California Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (“Rosenthal FDCPA”) are designed to protect people from unfair and unlawful debt collection. Debt collectors, including debt buyers and collection law firms, must follow both federal and California laws when collecting or attempting to collect a debt. They must be truthful and treat YOU with fairness, dignity, and respect.

    For example, debt collectors cannot:

  • use threats of violence or harm;
  • use obscene or profane language;
  • threaten that you will be arrested if you don’t pay your debt;
  • falsely claim that you have committed a crime;
  • repeatedly use the phone to annoy someone;
  • misrepresent the amount you owe;
  • attempt to collect a debt you do not owe;
  • give false credit information about you to anyone, including a credit reporting company;
  • try to collect any interest, fee, or other charge on top of the amount you owe unless it is permitted by the contract that created your debt or by California law;
  • Sent collection notices that are false or misleading.

    The FDCPA and Rosenthal FDCPA are laws designed to prevent debt collectors from engaging in unfair or unlawful conduct. Any debt collector, debt buyer, or collection law firm, who uses deceptive and abusive practices to collect, or attempt to collect a debt, may be liable for monetary damages, including actual and statutory damages under both the FDCPA and Rosenthal FDCPA. You may even be entitled to recover substantial money damages from the debt collector which has contacted you or sought to recover the debt from you. The FDCPA and Rosenthal FDCPA also allows for reasonable attorney’s fees, which means that if YOU prevail, the debt collector is liable to pay YOUR attorney’s fees. In some cases we can lower or even eliminate the debt entirely.

    • UNWANTED TELEPHONE CALLS

    Are You Getting Unwanted Telephone Calls, Collection Calls, Telemarketing Calls, or Text Messages On Your Cellular Telephone? Are You Getting Telemarking Calls On Your Home Phone?

    If you have received unwanted calls or text messages from creditors, debt collectors, telemarketers, or advertisers, you may have a claim for money damages under the Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA).

    The TCPA, which is encoded in 47 U.S.C. § 227 et seq., is a federal legislation dealing with restrictions on use of telephone equipment, such as an automatic telephone dialing system (“ATDS”). The purpose of the TCPA is to prohibit the use of ATDS to communicate with others by telephone in a manner that would be an invasion of privacy. It is unlawful for any person to make any calls to a cellular telephone (other than a call made for emergency purposes or made with the prior express consent of the called party) using any ATDS or an artificial or prerecorded voice. The TCPA also restricts telemarketers to call a person’s home phone, via an ATDS or an artificial or prerecorded voice, without prior express consent. Creditors, debt collectors, telemarketers, and advertisers often violate the TCPA.
